California wildfires have fundamentally reshaped the home insurance market. The devastating fires that have torn through communities across the state — from the foothills of Los Angeles to the hills above San Diego — have left thousands of homeowners scrambling for coverage as insurers retreat from the market.

If you live in or near a wildfire-prone area of California, here's what you need to know about finding home insurance in 2026.

## The Reality of the Current Market

Let's be direct: if you live in a high fire-hazard severity zone (HFHSZ), finding standard market home insurance is genuinely difficult right now. Major carriers have pulled back significantly. Getting a quote from a direct insurer or captive agent (one who only represents a single company) may result in a flat refusal — not because of anything you've done, but because of your ZIP code.

That said, coverage is available. Independent agents with access to multiple carriers — including specialty and surplus lines markets — can often find solutions that a single-carrier agent can't.

## What Is the California FAIR Plan?

The California FAIR Plan (Fair Access to Insurance Requirements) is a state-mandated insurer of last resort. If you can't find coverage in the standard market, you're entitled to FAIR Plan coverage regardless of where you live in California.

**What the FAIR Plan covers:**
- Fire, lightning, internal explosion
- Windstorm and hail (optional)
- The structure of your home up to $3 million

**What the FAIR Plan does NOT cover:**
- Liability (someone injured on your property)
- Theft
- Water damage (pipe leaks, etc.)
- Additional living expenses in most basic policies

This is why most insurance professionals recommend pairing a FAIR Plan policy with a "Difference in Conditions" (DIC) policy from a private carrier. The DIC fills in the gaps — liability, theft, water damage — at a relatively low additional cost.

## Private Market Alternatives to the FAIR Plan

Before defaulting to FAIR Plan, it's worth exhausting the private market. Several categories of carriers are actively writing in California wildfire-exposed areas:

**Surplus lines carriers** — Non-admitted insurers aren't subject to California's rate approval process, which means they can price wildfire risk appropriately and stay in the market. They're regulated differently but are legitimate, licensed carriers. Many quality surplus lines insurers are actively writing in California.

**Specialty homeowners carriers** — Some insurers specialize in high-value or high-risk properties and have the underwriting expertise and reinsurance structures to remain in challenging markets.

**Regional carriers** — Some smaller California-focused carriers never left the market and continue writing in areas that major nationals have abandoned.

An independent agent who works these markets daily will know which carriers are currently accepting applications in your specific ZIP code — something a Google search won't tell you.

## How to Make Your Home More Insurable

Insurers underwriting wildfire-exposed properties look closely at the physical characteristics of your home and its surroundings. Improvements that genuinely move the needle:

**Defensible space** — California law requires 100 feet of defensible space around structures. Zone 1 (0-30 feet): remove dead plants, dry leaves, and wood piles. Zone 2 (30-100 feet): cut grass low, space trees and shrubs. Documented defensible space is a real underwriting factor.

**Roof material** — Class A fire-rated roofing (composition shingles, metal, tile) significantly improves insurability vs. wood shake. If your roof is due for replacement, this is worth prioritizing.

**Ember-resistant vents** — Embers entering through vents cause a significant percentage of wildfire home ignitions. Ember-resistant vent screens are a low-cost, high-impact upgrade.

**Enclosed eaves and decks** — Open eaves and wood decks create ember catch points. Enclosing eaves and using non-combustible decking materials reduces risk.

**Home Hardening documentation** — Keep records and photos of all improvements. Some insurers — and the IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home program — offer formal recognition that can improve your position with underwriters.

## What Does Wildfire Home Insurance Cost in 2026?

Premiums in wildfire-exposed areas have increased substantially over the past several years, and they remain elevated. What you'll pay depends heavily on:

- Your specific location and fire hazard severity zone designation
- Your home's construction and fire-hardening measures
- The carrier (surplus lines pricing differs from admitted market)
- Your coverage limits and deductible

For homes in moderate-risk areas that retained standard market coverage, annual premiums of $3,000–$6,000 are common. For high-risk areas through surplus lines or FAIR Plan + DIC, $6,000–$15,000+ is not unusual for a mid-value home.

These numbers are uncomfortable, but they reflect the actual cost of insuring property against California's wildfire risk. Shopping across multiple carriers through an independent agent remains the best way to find the most competitive rate for your situation.

## What to Do If You've Been Non-Renewed

1. **Don't wait** — you have 75 days from your non-renewal notice. Start shopping immediately.
2. **Contact an independent agent** — they have market access that direct and captive agents don't.
3. **Document your property** — photos of your defensible space, roof, vents, and any fire-hardening work.
4. **Consider FAIR Plan + DIC as a backstop** — if the private market won't write you, this combination provides real protection.
5. **Don't go uninsured** — an uninsured wildfire loss can be financially catastrophic.
