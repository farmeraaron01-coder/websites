San Ysidro is one of the busiest land border crossings in the world, and a huge share of the cars in that line are San Diegans heading down for tacos in Tijuana, a weekend in Rosarito, or the wine valleys past Ensenada. Here's the question we get at our San Diego office every week, usually the day before someone leaves: *"My car insurance covers me in Mexico, right?"*

Mostly, no — and the part that matters isn't what your U.S. policy covers. It's what Mexican law recognizes.

## Why Your U.S. Policy Doesn't Work in Mexico

Two separate problems:

**1. Mexican law doesn't recognize U.S. liability coverage.** Mexico requires drivers to carry liability insurance issued by an insurer authorized in Mexico. Your California policy — no matter how good — doesn't satisfy that requirement. If you cause an accident, Mexican authorities treat you as uninsured.

**2. An accident in Mexico is handled under Mexican law**, where a serious accident can be treated as a criminal matter until fault and financial responsibility are sorted out. Drivers without valid Mexican insurance can be detained and their vehicle impounded while that happens. A Mexican policy comes with the pieces that resolve this: recognized liability coverage, plus **legal assistance and bail bond coverage** that are standard in Mexican auto policies precisely because of how the system works.

A footnote worth knowing: some U.S. carriers extend limited *physical damage* coverage a short distance into Mexico (often around 25 miles) — but that's damage to your own car only, it usually requires a Mexican liability policy to be in force anyway, and it does nothing for the legal-liability problem above. Don't cross on that alone.

## What a Mexico Auto Insurance Policy Includes

A proper tourist auto policy from a Mexico-authorized carrier typically covers:

- **Liability** (bodily injury and property damage) at limits valid in Mexico — we recommend $300,000+ combined single limit; Mexican courts calculate death liability in ways that make minimum limits genuinely risky
- **Physical damage and theft** for your own vehicle (optional but wise — vehicle theft is a real exposure)
- **Medical payments** for you and your passengers
- **Legal assistance and bail bond** — the coverage that keeps a bad day from becoming a detained one
- **Roadside assistance** in Mexico

Good policies also let you choose repairs in the U.S. rather than only in Mexico — worth confirming before you buy, and something we check by default.

## What It Costs from San Diego

- **Day trips:** short-term policies are priced per day — commonly in the $20–$50/day range depending on vehicle value and the coverage you choose. A Tijuana dinner run and a week in Ensenada are both easy to cover.
- **Frequent crossers:** if you go down more than a handful of times a year, an **annual Mexico policy** usually beats stacking daily policies — often comparable in total cost to just a few week-long trips.
- **Baja-specific notes:** if you're driving beyond the border zone (past Ensenada, or toward San Felipe), confirm your policy's territory. And leased or financed vehicles: check your lender's rules on taking the car into Mexico *before* the trip.

The variables that move price: vehicle value (for physical damage), liability limit, and trip length. Liability-only on an older car for a day trip is cheap; full coverage on a new truck for a month is not — but it's still a rounding error against what an uninsured accident in Mexico costs.

## Don't Buy It at the Border

You can buy Mexican insurance from booths near the crossing, but you're buying an unfamiliar policy under time pressure with no one to call afterward. As an independent San Diego agency we write Mexico tourist auto policies through established Mexico-authorized carriers *before* you leave — you cross with documents in hand, you know exactly what's covered, and if something happens, you call us, not a booth.

**Quick pre-trip checklist:**

1. Mexico auto policy purchased and printed (paper copies still matter at Mexican checkpoints)
2. Driver's license, registration, and passport
3. If the car is financed/leased: lender permission letter if required
4. Know your policy's claim phone number — claims in Mexico must typically be reported **before you leave Mexico**, ideally from the scene

That last one surprises people most: wait until you're back in San Diego to report the accident and coverage can be void. Call from the scene.
